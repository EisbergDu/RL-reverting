# Comparing Reinforcement Learning Methods in Simulated Mean-Reverting Stock Trading

## Abstract

This project studies the application of reinforcement learning to a simulated mean-reverting stock trading problem. The trading process is formulated as a continuous-action Markov Decision Process. At each time step, the agent observes the market state, chooses a position $A_t$, and receives a reward based on trading profit and regularization or transaction-cost penalties. The project compares three common continuous-control reinforcement learning algorithms: PPO, TD3, and SAC.

The stock price is generated by a simulated mean-reverting log-return process. Two reward functions are considered. Reward (1) penalizes absolute position size, while Reward (2) penalizes changes in position and therefore approximates transaction costs. For Reward (1), the optimal policy can be derived analytically because the action does not affect future price dynamics. This analytic policy is used as a benchmark for evaluating learned policies.

The high budget experiment uses 1000 independent Monte Carlo evaluation paths with evaluation horizon 80. The results show that TD3 and SAC generally outperform PPO. Under the baseline R1 oracle setting, SAC achieves a mean return of 485.16 and TD3 achieves 479.98, corresponding to 52.27% and 51.71% of the analytic benchmark. PPO obtains a mean return of -158.29. Under the baseline R2 oracle setting, SAC obtains a mean return of 464.66, slightly above TD3's 453.52, but SAC has a turnover of 6.98 compared with TD3's 2.80, indicating a more aggressive trading policy. Under the baseline R2 sequential setting, TD3 performs best with a mean return of 620.20 and low turnover.

Overall, the experiment shows that reinforcement learning can naturally model trading as a sequential decision problem, but performance depends strongly on reward design, data protocol, and market parameters. TD3 is the most robust method in this experiment, SAC is competitive in oracle settings but tends to trade more aggressively, and PPO is more conservative but less sample-efficient.

## 1. Background

Financial trading is a sequential decision-making problem. A trader observes market information over time, adjusts positions repeatedly, and receives profits or losses from future price movements. Unlike one-step price prediction, trading strategy learning requires choosing a position that balances expected reward, risk exposure, and transaction costs over time.

Reinforcement learning provides a natural framework for this problem. In this project:

- The state represents observable market information.
- The action is the current trading position.
- The reward combines trading profit with regularization or transaction-cost penalties.
- The policy maps market states to trading actions.
- The objective is to maximize expected discounted total reward.

This project uses simulated data rather than real stock data. This choice makes the experiment more controlled. Market parameters such as mean-reversion speed, volatility, and penalty strength can be changed directly. Independent evaluation paths can also be generated from the simulator. Most importantly, Reward (1) admits an analytic benchmark, which helps verify whether a learned policy captures the correct structure.

The goal is not to build a deployable trading strategy. Instead, the project demonstrates how a financial trading task can be formalized as a reinforcement learning problem and how different RL algorithms behave under the same simulated environment.

## 2. Market Model

Let $S_t$ be the stock price with initial value $S_0 = 1$. The log-return process $L_t$ follows:


$$
L_{t+1} = (1 - \kappa) L_t + \sigma Z_t,\qquad Z_t \sim N(0, 1)
$$


$$
S_{t+1} = S_t \exp(L_{t+1})
$$


Here, $\kappa \in (0,1)$ controls the speed of mean reversion, $\sigma > 0$ controls volatility, and $Z_t$ is an independent standard normal random variable. Larger $\kappa$ makes returns revert to zero faster, while larger $\sigma$ creates more volatile price paths.

The action $A_t$ is the number of shares held at time $t$. Positive values represent long positions, negative values represent short positions, and zero represents no position. Since $A_t$ is continuous, this is a continuous-control problem. In implementation, actions are clipped to a bounded interval to avoid unstable extreme positions.

## 3. MDP Formulation

For Reward (1), the state is:


$$
s_t = (\log S_t, L_t)
$$


For Reward (2), the state is:


$$
s_t = (\log S_t, L_t, A_{t-1})
$$


Reward (2) must include the previous action $A_{t-1}$, because its cost term depends on the position change $A_t - A_{t-1}$. Without $A_{t-1}$, the agent cannot infer the cost of changing positions from the current state alone, and the Markov property would be violated.

The objective is:


$$
\text{maximize }\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t} \gamma^t R_t\right]
$$


The `mean_return` reported in this paper is the Monte Carlo estimate of this discounted total reward. It is not a stock return percentage.

## 4. Reward Design

### 4.1 Reward (1): Position Regularization

Reward (1) is:


$$
R_t = A_t (S_{t+1} - S_t) - \lambda A_t^2
$$


The first term is trading profit. A long position gains when the price rises, and a short position gains when the price falls. The second term penalizes large absolute positions, preventing the policy from taking unlimited leverage.

The key advantage of Reward (1) is analytical tractability. Since the action does not affect future price dynamics, the optimal action can be solved period by period. This analytic policy provides a benchmark for learned policies.

### 4.2 Reward (2): Position-Change Penalty

Reward (2) is:


$$
R_t = A_t (S_{t+1} - S_t) - \lambda (A_t - A_{t-1})^2 S_t
$$


Unlike Reward (1), this reward penalizes changes in position rather than the position size itself. It is closer to a stylized transaction-cost model, because frequent trading generates costs in real markets.

Under Reward (2), a reasonable policy should be smoother. If the previous position is already large, the agent should avoid making unnecessary large changes unless the market signal is strong enough.

## 5. Data Protocols

### 5.1 Simulation Oracle

In the simulation oracle setting, the algorithm can repeatedly generate new independent episodes from the known simulator. This is an idealized setting, similar to having unlimited access to a market simulator. It provides broader data coverage and generally makes training easier.

### 5.2 Sequential Data

In the sequential data setting, training data comes from a fixed price path. This is closer to historical financial data, where one cannot generate unlimited independent market trajectories. The data are more correlated and less diverse, making training more challenging.

## 6. Reinforcement Learning Algorithms

### 6.1 PPO

PPO, or Proximal Policy Optimization, is an on-policy policy-gradient method. It collects rollouts using the current policy and updates the policy using advantage estimates. Its core idea is the clipped objective:


$$
\min\left(r_t A_t,\; \operatorname{clip}(r_t, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A_t\right)
$$


where $r_t$ is the probability ratio between the new and old policies. The clipping mechanism prevents overly large policy updates and improves training stability.

In this project, PPO uses an actor network to output a continuous action distribution and a critic network to estimate the value function. PPO is stable but sample-inefficient because rollout data are mainly used for the current policy update and are not reused extensively.

In the high budget results, PPO is generally conservative. It has low turnover and smaller average positions, but its mean return is weak. Under the baseline R1 oracle setting, PPO obtains a mean return of -158.29, indicating that it fails to learn an effective trading policy in this setup.

### 6.2 TD3

TD3, or Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient, is an off-policy deterministic actor-critic method. The actor outputs a deterministic action $A_t = \mu(s_t)$, and the critics estimate $Q(s_t, A_t)$.

TD3 has three important mechanisms:

1. Twin critics: two Q networks are trained, and the smaller target value is used to reduce overestimation.
2. Delayed policy updates: the actor is updated less frequently than the critics, making actor updates more reliable.
3. Target policy smoothing: clipped noise is added to target actions to reduce overfitting to sharp Q-function peaks.

TD3 also uses a replay buffer, which allows it to reuse past transitions and improve sample efficiency. In the high budget results, TD3 is especially robust under sequential data. It achieves a mean return of 601.52 under baseline R1 sequential and 620.20 under baseline R2 sequential, both the best results in their settings.

### 6.3 SAC

SAC, or Soft Actor-Critic, is an off-policy stochastic actor-critic method. It adds an entropy term to the reward objective:


$$
\text{expected reward} + \alpha \times \text{entropy}
$$


This encourages exploration and prevents the policy from becoming deterministic too quickly. In noisy financial environments, this entropy regularization can help the policy explore a wider range of trading behaviors.

In the high budget results, SAC is strong in oracle settings. Under baseline R1 oracle, SAC obtains a mean return of 485.16, slightly above TD3. Under baseline R2 oracle, SAC obtains a mean return of 464.66, also slightly above TD3. However, SAC has a turnover of 6.98 under R2 oracle, much higher than TD3's 2.80, suggesting a more aggressive trading policy.

## 12. Algorithm Comparison Summary

PPO is stable and conservative, but it is the weakest method in this experiment. Its turnover is usually low, but its mean return is often negative.

TD3 is the most robust method overall. It performs especially well under sequential data settings, suggesting that its replay buffer, twin critics, and deterministic actor are effective for fixed-path trading data.

SAC is competitive in oracle settings and slightly outperforms TD3 under baseline R1 oracle and R2 oracle. However, SAC can have high turnover, especially under R2 oracle, indicating more aggressive trading behavior.

Overall, TD3 is more robust, SAC explores more and performs well in oracle settings, and PPO is more conservative but less profitable under the current training setup.

## 13. Limitations and Future Work

This project uses a simulated market and omits many real-world trading frictions, including bid-ask spreads, slippage, market impact, capital constraints, and market nonstationarity. Therefore, the learned policies should not be interpreted as directly tradable strategies.

The experiment uses two training seeds. Although the high budget evaluation uses 1000 Monte Carlo paths, training randomness can still affect the conclusions. A more rigorous study should use more seeds and report confidence intervals.

The main metric is mean discounted total reward. Real investment strategies should also be evaluated using Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, tail risk, VaR/CVaR, and capital usage.

Some parameter sets produce extreme reward magnitudes, showing that the exponential price model can generate extreme paths under high volatility or slow mean reversion. Future work could add price clipping, log-return-based rewards, or risk-adjusted objectives.

Future extensions include using real stock or ETF data, applying walk-forward validation, adding realistic transaction costs, comparing more algorithms such as DDPG, A2C, or discrete-action DQN, and performing hyperparameter ablations over learning rate, network width, batch size, and replay buffer size.

## 14. Conclusion

This project demonstrates a complete reinforcement learning pipeline for a financial trading task: constructing a simulated market environment, defining states, actions, and rewards, implementing PPO, TD3, and SAC, evaluating learned policies through Monte Carlo simulation, and visualizing policy behavior.

The high budget results show that TD3 and SAC generally outperform PPO. SAC is competitive in oracle settings but has higher turnover, TD3 is more robust under sequential data, and PPO is conservative but weak in return.

The main contribution of the project is to clearly demonstrate how financial trading can be formalized as a reinforcement learning problem and how different RL algorithms learn different trading behaviors under the same environment.
